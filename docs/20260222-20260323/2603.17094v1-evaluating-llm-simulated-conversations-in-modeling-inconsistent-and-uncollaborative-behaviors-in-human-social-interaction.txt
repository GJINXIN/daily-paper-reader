## Evaluating LLM-Simulated Conversations in Modeling Inconsistent and Uncollaborative Behaviors in Human Social Interaction 

Ryo Kamoi 2† , Ameya Godbole 3† , Longqi Yang 1, Rui Zhang 2, Mengting Wan 1, Pei Zhou 1

1Microsoft Corporation, 2Penn State University, 3University of Southern California 

{ryokamoi, rmz5227}@psu.edu, {mengtwan, zhoupei}@microsoft.com 

Abstract 

Simulating human conversations using large language models (LLMs) has emerged as a scal-able methodology for modeling human social interaction. However, simulating human con-versations is challenging because they inher-ently involve inconsistent and uncollaborative behaviors, such as misunderstandings and in-terruptions. Analysis comparing inconsistent and uncollaborative behaviors in human- and LLM-generated conversations remains limited, although reproducing these behaviors is inte-gral to simulating human-like and complex so-cial interaction. In this work, we introduce COCOEVAL , an evaluation framework that an-alyzes LLM-simulated conversations by detect-ing 10 types of inconsistent and uncollaborative behaviors at the turn level using an LLM-as-a-Judge. Using C OCOEVAL , we evaluate GPT-4.1, GPT-5.1, and Claude Opus 4 by comparing the frequencies of detected behaviors in conver-sations simulated by each model and in human conversations across academic, business, and governmental meetings, as well as debates. Our analysis shows that (1) under vanilla prompt-ing, LLM-simulated conversations exhibit far fewer inconsistent and uncollaborative behav-iors than human conversations; (2) prompt en-gineering does not provide reliable control over these behaviors, as our results show that differ-ent prompts lead to their under- or overproduc-tion; and (3) supervised fine-tuning on human conversations can lead LLMs to overproduce a narrow set of behaviors, such as repetition. Our findings highlight the difficulty of simulating human conversations, raising concerns about the use of LLMs as a proxy for human social interaction.

However, greater consistency or col-laborativeness does not necessarily indicate a bet-ter simulation, because human conversations inher-ently involve inconsistent behaviors such as misun-derstandings and repetition, as well as uncollabora-tive behaviors such as interruptions and disagree-ments (Grice, 1975; Schegloff, 1992; Pickering and Garrod, 2004). Simulating these behaviors is essential for generating human-like conversations and capturing the complexity of human social in-teraction. Nevertheless, it has not been sufficiently analyzed whether LLM-based simulations can re-produce inconsistent and uncollaborative behaviors with frequencies and distributions comparable to those observed in human conversations. To address this gap, we introduce C OCOEVAL ,an evaluation framework for analyzing inconsistent and uncollaborative behaviors in LLM-simulated conversations. The framework consists of an evalu-ation scheme and a benchmark. First, we propose a fine-grained evaluation that detects inconsistent and uncollaborative behaviors at the turn level. We define 10 types of inconsistent and uncollaborative behaviors based on taxonomies for dialogue gen-eration and conversation simulation (Higashinaka et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023) and use an LLM-as-a-Judge to identify turns exhibiting each type of behavior. In this evaluation, a simulation is consid-ered successful if these turn-level behaviors occur with frequencies and distributions comparable to those in human conversations. This evaluation com-plements a commonly used coarse-grained LLM-as-a-Judge approach that assigns conversation-level scores (Zhou et al., 2024b; Teng et al., 2024), such as consistency and collaborativeness rated on a 1-10 scale. Second, we introduce the C OCOEVAL -BENCH benchmark to evaluate LLMs on the task of generating 30-turn conversation continuations given conversational metadata and history. It is cre-ated from real-world human conversations in QM-Sum (Zhong et al., 2021), NCPC (NCPC, 2024), SIM (Fan et al., 2024), and IQ2 (Zhang et al., 2016), spanning business, academic, and governmental meetings as well as debates. Using C OCOEVAL , we evaluate GPT-4.1, GPT-5.1, and Claude Opus 4 by comparing conversa-tions simulated by each model with human con-versations. The results highlight the difficulty of reproducing inconsistent and uncollaborative be-haviors with frequencies comparable to those ob-served in human conversations, as shown in Fig-ure 1. (a) First, under vanilla prompting, LLMs generate conversations with far fewer inconsistent and uncollaborative behaviors than human con-versations. (b) Second, prompt engineering does not provide reliable control over these behaviors. LLMs overproduce inconsistent and uncollabora-tive behaviors when explicitly instructed to include them at frequencies observed in human conversa-tions. (c) Third, supervised fine-tuning on human conversations fails to capture the diversity of their inconsistent and uncollaborative behaviors. Instead, fine-tuned GPT-4.1 overproduces a narrow range of behaviors such as repetition and redundancy. (d) Fi-nally, generating an entire conversation in a single LLM call results in substantially fewer inconsistent and uncollaborative behaviors than generating each turn in a separate call. In summary, our analysis indicates that incon-sistent and uncollaborative behaviors in LLM-simulated conversations exhibit different frequen-cies and distributions from those in human con-versations. Their frequencies are sensitive to the simulation setup, making them difficult to control. These findings highlight the difficulty of simulating human conversations and raise concerns about us-ing LLMs as proxies for human social interaction. Our main contributions are as follows: • We introduce C OCOEVAL , a framework for eval-uating how well LLM-simulated conversations can reproduce inconsistent and uncollaborative behaviors inherent in human conversations. • We introduce a fine-grained evaluation scheme that detects 10 types of inconsistent and uncol-laborative behaviors at the turn level. • We show that, in a vanilla prompting setting, LLM-simulated conversations include far fewer inconsistent and uncollaborative behaviors com-pared to human conversations. • Our analysis shows that neither prompt engineer-ing nor supervised fine-tuning provides reliable control of inconsistent and uncollaborative be-haviors in LLM-simulated conversations.

3 COCOEVAL 

We introduce C OCOEVAL , an evaluation frame-work for analyzing LLM-simulated conversations with a focus on inconsistent and uncollaborative behaviors. C OCOEVAL consists of an evaluation scheme (§3.1) and a benchmark (§3.2). Human conversations inherently contain inconsistent and uncollaborative behaviors (Schegloff, 1992; Grice, 1975; Pickering and Garrod, 2004), so higher con-sistency or collaborativeness does not necessarily indicate a better simulation. Instead, a successful simulation should generate inconsistent and uncol-laborative behaviors with frequencies and distri-butions comparable to those in human conversa-tions. The evaluation scheme and benchmark of COCOEVAL are designed to facilitate comparison between human- and LLM-generated conversations with respect to these behaviors. 

3.1 Evaluation Scheme 

COCOEVAL evaluates the consistency and col-laborativeness of LLM-simulated conversations using two complementary LLM-as-a-Judge ap-proaches: overall and fine-grained evaluation. 

Overall evaluation. The overall evaluation as-signs two conversation-level scores, one for con-sistency and one for collaborativeness, each on a 1-10 scale. This form of evaluation is commonly used in conversation simulation (Zhou et al., 2024b; Teng et al., 2024). Our prompt for LLM-as-a-Judge includes definitions of consistency and collabora-3tiveness, together with scoring rubrics describing the criteria for each score range. The definitions of consistency and collaborativeness in C OCOEVAL 

follow prior discussions in conversation evaluation. 

Consistency in this work includes coherence with previous utterances by all speakers, adherence to the specified persona, and correctness with respect to world knowledge and social norms (Park et al., 2023; Frisch and Giulianelli, 2024; Abdulhai et al., 2025), as well as the avoidance of unnecessary re-dundancy and repetition (Higashinaka et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2025). Collaborativeness is defined as the extent to which a speaker’s contributions support shared group objectives and facilitate the goals of other participants (Zhou et al., 2024b; Pi-atti et al., 2024; Piedrahita et al., 2025; Nonomura and Mori, 2025), without engaging in actions that delay or impede progress and agreement. 

Fine-grained evaluation. To provide a more de-tailed and interpretable evaluation, we introduce a fine-grained evaluation that detects 10 types of in-consistent and uncollaborative behaviors at the turn level. We derive 10 types of inconsistent and un-collaborative behaviors shown in Table 1 by adapt-ing nine types of behaviors from evaluation tax-onomies in dialogue generation (Higashinaka et al., 2021; Mahajan et al., 2022), and we added “persis-tent disagreement” that reflects collaborativeness in negotiation and debates (Wang et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2025). For each category, the LLM-as-a-Judge is instructed to detect turns that exhibit the corresponding behaviors and count the number of occurrences in each conversation. 

Agreement between human evaluation and LLM-as-a-Judge. We collect human evaluations with two annotators to assess agreement with the LLM-as-a-Judge. The human annotators re-ceive the same instructions used for the LLM-as-a-Judge and perform manual annotation. We evaluate OpenAI o4-mini (OpenAI, 2025c) and Claude Opus 4 (Anthropic, 2025), and select o4-mini because it shows higher agreement with hu-man evaluation. For overall evaluation , the two annotators evaluate the overall consistency and col-laborativeness of 54 conversations generated by humans and GPT-4.1 on a 1-10 scale. The aver-age Spearman’s rank correlation coefficients be-tween human scores and LLM-as-a-Judge scores using o4-mini are 0.301 for consistency and 0.380 for collaborativeness. 3.2 COCOEVAL BENCH 

We introduce the C OCOEVAL BENCH benchmark for evaluating LLMs on the conversation continua-tion task shown in Figure 2. The benchmark covers real-world scenarios spanning business, academic, and governmental meetings, as well as debates. 

Evaluation task. The conversation continuation task in C OCOEVAL BENCH requires LLMs to gen-erate continuations that follow the provided con-versational metadata and history. The input con-sists of metadata M containing the conversation goals and participant information, the 30 preced-ing turns x = [ x−30 , . . . , x −1], and a paragraph-length summary S of the conversation prior to x.Each turn in x is represented as a speaker-utterance pair xi = ( pi, u i). Given this input, LLMs are instructed to generate 30 continuation turns y =[y1, . . . , y 30 ] ∼ LLM( M, S, x ), where each turn is a pair of a speaker and an utterance. To assess the simulation of long-term dynamics in human conversations, our setup generates 30-turn continu-ations, a larger number of turns than in most prior work (Zhou et al., 2024b; Bianchi et al., 2024). Benchmark creation. To create the benchmark for the conversation con-tinuation task, we first use o4-mini to extract infor-mation from conversations in the source datasets to generate conversation-level metadata M . Next, we randomly select a start point for the conversa-tion continuation task and extract the preceding 30 turns as history x and the following 30 turns as reference continuation r. We then generate a paragraph-length summary S of the conversation preceding the 30-turn history x using o4-mini.

5.3 SFT on Human Conversations 

The overall evaluation in Table 3 shows that GPT-4.1 fine-tuned on human conversations with SFT exhibits levels of consistency and collaborativeness comparable to those of human conversations, espe-cially when generating one turn per call. However, the fine-grained evaluation in Figure 4g shows that the frequencies and distributions of inconsistent and uncollaborative behaviors do not match those of human conversations. Specifically, fine-tuned GPT-4.1 overproduces repetition and redundancy, whereas human conversations display more diverse behavioral patterns. These results indicate that even when fine-tuned directly on human conversations via SFT, LLM-simulated conversations still deviate from human conversations. 

Answer to RQ3 about SFT 

SFT on human conversations can lead LLMs to overproduce a narrow subset of inconsistent behaviors, remaining misaligned with human social interaction, which exhibits a diverse range of inconsistent and uncollaborative behaviors. 

5.4 Generating Multiple Turns Per LLM Call 

The results in Table 3 and Figure 4 consistently show that generating multiple turns per LLM call, particularly 30 turns, introduces a strong bias to-ward reducing inconsistent and uncollaborative be-haviors. In particular, Figure 4d shows that generat-ing 30 turns in a single LLM call almost eliminates the effect of taxonomy-guided prompting, whereas it substantially increases inconsistent and uncollab-orative behaviors when one turn is generated in a single call. 6 Analysis and Discussion 

Quantitative analysis. To further analyze the re-sults in Section 5, we conduct a manual analysis of inconsistent and uncollaborative behaviors in LLM-simulated conversations. With supervised fine-tuning for GPT-4.1, the fine-grained evaluation in Figure 4g shows a high frequency of repetitive and redundant utterances in generated conversations. Manual analysis suggests that this result reflects unnatural repetition in LLM-simulated conversa-tions rather than human-like inconsistent behaviors. Table 4 presents an example generated by the fine-tuned GPT-4.1 (generating one turn per call) on QMSum Product, in which the same speaker re-peatedly expresses agreement with prior utterances. This example suggests that SFT on human conver-sations can introduce unnatural repetitive behavior. With prompt engineering , the fine-grained eval-uation in Figure 4e shows that taxonomy-guided prompting produces overly large numbers of incon-sistent and uncollaborative behaviors when gener-ating one turn per call. Manual analysis suggests that this result reflects behaviors that deviate from those observed in human conversations. Table 5 presents an example generated by GPT-4.1 with the taxonomy-guided prompt (generating one turn per call) on QMSum Product. The example exhibits persistent disagreement, with two speakers dom-inating the conversation despite the presence of multiple participants. This example illustrates the sensitivity of uncollaborative behaviors to prompt engineering, even though our prompt explicitly in-structs “to mimic the communication style and con-tent in the metadata and the conversation history.”

7 Conclusion 

We introduce C OCOEVAL , an evaluation frame-work for analyzing inconsistent and uncollabora-tive behaviors in LLM-simulated conversations. Our analysis shows that LLMs with vanilla prompt-ing exhibit far fewer inconsistent and uncollabora-tive behaviors than human conversations, and that neither prompt engineering nor supervised fine-tuning reliably controls these behaviors. These results highlight the challenge of simulating human conversations, particularly in reproducing their in-herent inconsistency and uncollaborativeness. 8Limitations 

Dataset diversity. Our analysis is constrained by the limited availability of publicly accessible datasets containing real-world multi-party conver-sations. Existing datasets are heavily skewed to-ward governmental meetings, while datasets from other domains such as business meetings remain scarce. Collecting datasets from a broader range of real-world settings would support the study of social interaction across more diverse scenarios. 

Language scope. Our study focuses on conversa-tions in English.

A.1 Source Datasets 

COCOEVAL BENCH is created from six public con-versational datasets. Table 6 shows the sources of the datasets. 

A.2 Benchmark Creation 

Before the process described in Section 3.2, we clean up undesirable artifacts in QMSum Product and Academic. QMSum Product and Academic include transcripts that preserve all details of spo-ken meetings and include disfluencies, fillers, and unnecessary repetitions. To avoid transcription ar-tifacts from influencing our analysis, we clean the utterances by instructing o4-mini to remove disflu-encies while preserving the remaining content. 

A.3 Input Length and Token Usage 

In the conversation continuation task in C OCO-EVAL BENCH , the input includes approximately 4k tokens, and the output includes about 50 to-kens per turn on average. Therefore, for exam-ple, when generating 30 turns under the setting of generating one turn per LLM call, the total input tokens amount to approximately 4k×30 = 120 k to-kens, and the total output tokens amount to about 

50 × 30 = 1 .5k tokens. The evaluation set of COCOEVAL BENCH includes 50 × 6 = 300 con-versations, so in total this setting requires approx-imately 120 k × 300 ≃ 36 M input tokens and 

1.5k × 300 = 450 k output tokens.

B.1 Setup of Human Evaluation 

For the evaluation of the agreement between hu-man evaluation and the LLM-as-a-Judge in Sec-tion 3.1, two PhD students working on NLP re-search conducted the human evaluation. They were provided with the same instructions as those used in the prompt for the LLM-as-a-Judge, and the an-notators were not informed whether the conversa-tions were generated by humans or by LLMs. The inputs (M, S, x ) are randomly selected from the evaluation set of C OCOEVAL and evenly sampled from instances created from the six source datasets. They are informed about this study and provide consent for their annotations to be used. 

Overall evaluation. Two human annotators man-ually evaluate the overall consistency and collabo-rativeness of 54 conversation continuations gener-ated by humans, GPT-4.1, and fine-tuned GPT-4.1, where LLMs generate 30 turns per call. They as-sign scores on a 1-10 scale by referring to the in-structions and rubrics provided to LLM-as-a-Judge. 

Fine-grained evaluation. Two human annotators manually detect the 10 types of inconsistent and un-collaborative behaviors in 1,080 turns from 36 hu-man conversations and conversation continuations simulated by GPT-4.1 (generating 30 turns per call). The annotators provide the indices of turns that ex-hibit each type of behavior. 

B.2 Model Selection for LLM-as-a-Judge 

This paper uses o4-mini as the primary LLM-as-a-Judge model, as it shows reasonable alignment with human evaluation. We also evaluate Claude Opus 4 as an LLM-as-a-Judge, but select o4-mini due to the misalignment between Claude Opus 4 and hu-man evaluation. For overall evaluation, the average Spearman’s rank correlation coefficients between Claude Opus 4 and human scores are -0.004 for consistency and 0.171 for collaborativeness. For fine-grained evaluation, the average agreement be-tween Claude Opus 4 and human evaluation is 0.121 in Cohen’s kappa and 0.093 in Matthews correlation coefficient. In both evaluations, agree-ment between Claude Opus 4 and human evalua-tion is substantially lower than both human-human agreement and the agreement between human eval-uation and o4-mini. Specifically, the overall eval-uation by Claude Opus 4 follows a different trend from that of human evaluation and o4-mini, tending to be more sensitive to repetition and redundancy and assigning lower scores to conversations gen-erated with one turn per call and fine-tuned GPT-4.1. In the fine-grained evaluation, Claude Opus 4 often detects inconsistent and uncollaborative be-haviors more aggressively, showing recall against human detection comparable to o4-mini (30.5 by Claude Opus 4 and 42.7 by o4-mini) but with lower precision (7.1 by Claude Opus 4 and 20.2 by o4-mini). 

B.3 Details of Human-LLM Agreement 

Table 7 presents detailed results on the agree-ment between human evaluation and the LLM-as-a-Judge using o4-mini for the fine-grained evaluation. The overall values in the last row are computed by concatenating the binary detection results across all categories. The results show that the number of 12 inconsistent and uncollaborative behaviors identi-fied by the o4-mini largely follows the trends ob-served in the human evaluation, although o4-mini appears more sensitive in detecting these behaviors and often identifies more occurrences than human annotators. 

B.4 Coverage of Fine-grained Evaluation 

To verify that the 10 types of behaviors used in the fine-grained evaluation of C OCOEVAL cover most inconsistent and uncollaborative behaviors in human conversations, we instructed the human an-notators to identify any behaviors not captured by these categories when conducting human evalua-tion for the fine-grained evaluation. In the reviewed conversations, the annotators did not identify any behaviors outside the 10 types of behaviors.
