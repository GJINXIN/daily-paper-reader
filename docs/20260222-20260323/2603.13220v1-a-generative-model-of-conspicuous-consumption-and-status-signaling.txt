# A Generative Model of Conspicuous Consumption and Status Signaling 

Logan Cross 1, Jordi Grau-Moya 1, William A. Cunningham 1,2 , Alexander Sasha Vezhnevets 1 and Joel Z. Leibo 1 

> 1Google DeepMind, 2University of Toronto

Status signaling drives human behavior and the allocation of scarce resources such as mating oppor-tunities, yet the generative mechanisms governing how specific goods, signals, or behaviors acquire prestige remain a puzzle. Classical frameworks, such as Costly Signaling Theory, treat preferences as fixed and struggle to explain how semiotic meaning changes based on context or drifts dynamically over time, occasionally reaching tipping points. In this work, we propose a computational theory of status grounded in the theory of appropriateness, positing that status symbols emerge endogenously through a feedback loop of social observation and predictive pattern completion. We validate this theory using simulations of groups of Large Language Model (LLM)-based agents in the Concordia framework. By experimentally manipulating social visibility within naturalistic agent daily routines, we demonstrate that social interactions transform functional demand into status-seeking behavior. We observe the emergence of price run-ups and positive price elasticity (Veblen effects) for both real-world luxury items and procedurally generated synthetic goods, ruling out pretraining bias as the sole driver. Furthermore, we demonstrate that "influencer" agents can drive the endogenous formation of distinct subcultures through targeted sanctioning, and find that similar social influence effects generalize to non-monetary signaling behaviors. This work provides a generative bridge between micro-level cognition and macro-level economic and sociological phenomena, offering a new methodology for forecasting how cultural conventions emerge from interaction. 

# 1. Introduction 

Status signaling fundamentally drives human behavior, wherein individuals take actions to advertise otherwise unobservable traits like wealth, fitness, or group allegiance. This drive is adaptive and evolutionarily conserved, as status is a relative ranking that mediates the allocation of scarce resources such as attention, allies, and mating opportunities (Grafen, 1990; Zahavi, 1975). Beyond resource allocation, these displays serve a semiotic function: they act as a system of social classification that allows individuals to distinguish in-group members and infer the latent characteristics of others (Bourdieu, 1979; Jordan et al., 2016; Simmel, 1904; Spence, 1978; Veblen and Howells, 1899). Modern evolutionary accounts suggest that these signals also facilitate cultural learning by helping individuals identify prestigious models to imitate (Henrich, 2015; Henrich and Gil-White, 2001). Yet, the generative mechanism governing these displays remains a puzzle. Why do some goods, behaviors, or ideas become potent status symbols while others fade, and why do their meanings shift so dynamically across cultures and time (Barker et al., 2019)? Researchers have struggled to empirically isolate the mechanisms, as these signals emerge organically and are socially constructed. Field data about the consumption of status goods confound prestige, marketing, and social diffusion (Hartmann et al., 2008; Manski, 1993). Experiments in the lab struggle with ecological validity and making status and visibility realistic without losing control (Harrison and List, 2004; Levitt and List, 2007). Understanding not just what functions as a signal but also how signals emerge, shift, and propagate is a central problem in social science. Costly Signaling Theory (CST) explains conspicuous consumption by treating expenditure as a credible signal of status because the cost is hard to fake for low status individuals (Grafen, 1990; Zahavi, 1975). However, even though CST explains why costly signals are honest and have stable equilibria, high production cost is neither necessary nor sufficient to create a status signal (Barker et al., 2019; Lachmann et al., 2001). Research on neutral markers demonstrates that nearly costless behaviors can emerge as in-group signaling conventions. For example accents, slang, religious markings, conventional in-group badges, and moralized reposts of political news—are nearly costless to produce yet remain reliable social signals (Barker et al., 2019; Cohen, 2012; Ghezae et al., 2024; Samarasinghe et al., 2019; Tajfel et al., 1971; Tibbetts and Dale, 2004). These signals solve a coordination problem in which the value of a symbol emerges through equilibrium selection: once a community converges on an arbitrary marker, in-group members imitate that marker over alternatives (Centola et al., 2018; Lachmann et al., 2001; Lewis, 1969; Skyrms, 2010). Social imitation of arbitrary symbols and rituals serves as a reliable learning heuristic as humans are embedded in complex environments where knowledge is opaque and culturally inherited (Henrich, 2015). Moreover, high cost is not sufficient for status, as the semiotic value of a signal is often subject to rapid cultural drift. Expensive goods frequently lose their signaling power because they become accessible to the ’wrong’ groups or fail to align with shifting aesthetic norms, rendering them passé despite their continued expense (Berger and Heath, 2008; Bourdieu, 1979; Leibenstein, 1950; Pesendorfer, 1995; Simmel, 1904). A unified model of signaling must therefore explain why costly signals sometimes work, why cheap signals often work, and why entire cultural regimes of signaling can shift rapidly as conventions change. We propose such a model unified model grounded in the theory of appropriateness, positing that status symbols emerge endogenously through a feedback loop of social observation and imitation via predictive pattern completion (Bowles, 1998; Leibo et al., 2024, 2025b). Predictive pattern completion conceptualizes humans as culturally trained generative models who produce actions that are appropriate for their context by developing a habitus — an intuitive sense for the “rules of the game” coined by sociologist Pierre Bourdieu (Bourdieu, 1979). In this view, signals acquire value through social diffusion: individuals observe peers, update expectations about what "people like us" do, and imitate the signaling behaviors that align with that shared context. We operationalize our theory using multi-agent societies of generative agents in Concordia (Anthis et al., 2025; Park et al., 2023; Vezhnevets et al., 2023, 2025). As previous work validated that these Large Language Model (LLM)-based agents can reproduce human signaling dynamics in controlled experiments (Cross et al., 2025), we scale these mechanisms to a naturalistic setting to observe the endogenous emergence of status signals. This approach allows us to create ecologically-valid yet precisely controllable social environments, where we can isolate the essential ingredients for status dynamics to emerge without identification problems (Manski, 1993). It enables us to manipulate who talks to whom, what is publicly visible, and how choices are made, while measuring the emergent outcomes and revealed preferences of the agents (Samuelson, 1938). In our experiments, LLM agents live daily lives consistent with synthetic personas by observing daily events and making decisions that have private and social effects. Agents can buy food, gadgets, clothing, and accessories in a marketplace. The agents have social interactions—first dates—where their visible outfit/accessories are sampled from inventories, and they chat freely about their lives. With this social layer turned on, we observe high demand and price run-ups for status goods (e.g., Chanel handbags, Rolex watches) and for a toy collectible (Labubu) that is currently trending internationally but had not been well known prior to the base model’s knowledge cutoff (Lawrence, 2025; Yang and Li, 2025). The hype for Labubu was so high that we observed positive price elasticity over time, meaning Labubu became a Veblen good. Without the social layer, prices remain static and agents purchase status goods at a significantly lower rate. We reproduced this effect with synthetically generated status goods, ruling out the possibility that these results are driven by the brand prestige ingrained in the LLMs prior. Observing these price-runs and Veblen effects in our "field data" presents a identification problem: disentangling whether a high price causes high demand (per Costly Signaling Theory) or if socially constructed demand drives up the price through extensive hype. Our fixed-price control resolves this ambiguity as status purchases increased even more with fixed initial prices, demonstrating that social contagion—not signal cost—causally drives consumption. To test the generality of this social mechanism, we extended our framework to reproduce human examples of non-monetary signaling that are documented in the literature using procedurally gener-ated status signaling simulations (Barker et al., 2019; Glazer and Konrad, 1996; Jordan and Rand, 2020; Skyrms, 2010). Given a trait to signal such as group identity, political allegiance, or altruism, we synthetically generate diverse scenarios that evaluate whether the agents prefer to produce a behavior that emits a social signal or a private behavior. Similarly to the marketplace simulations, signaling behaviors increased in conditions where agents socially interact and are exposed to these signals from others. When we hold everything else constant and remove the social life, the desire for these status symbols reliably decreases.

# 2. Theoretical Framework: Predictive Pattern Completion as the Generative Mechanism of Status Signaling 

Status signalling is a dynamic process of social communication and cultural construction. We define a signaling behavior as an action that serves as an intersubjective symbol, allowing observers to infer latent, desirable traits from the signal. Through a cycle of imitation and peer sanctioning, humans learn to produce symbols in contexts where they have previously observed them, eventually internalizing these patterns as automatic habits that sometimes persist even in private (Jordan and Rand, 2020; Leibo et al., 2024, 2025b). As this process spreads, social contagion leads to a co-

Over time in a culture, these accumulated signals coalesce into a dynamic semiotic code—a complex symbolic language that allows individuals to implicitly broadcast their group identity and social standing. What makes a good signal? From a classical view, evolutionary pressures select a status signal to pragmatically assist cooperation and social interaction (Alexander, 1987; Boyd and Richerson, 1989; Gintis et al., 2001; Grafen, 1990; Henrich, 2009; Nowak and Sigmund, 1998; Young, 2015; Zahavi, 1975). Signals help produce stable cooperative equilibria by enabling reputation systems that efficiently mediate the allocation of scarce resources (Jordan, 2023). Partner choice is the canonical example of these status markets. In evolutionary biology, the peacock’s tail is a costly, wasteful handicap that honestly signals genetic fitness to a potential mate (Zahavi, 1975). In human contexts, conspicuous consumption, such as buying expensive luxury cars or clothing, can function as a similar sexual signaling system (Miller, 2001). Our simulation’s "first date" context is a direct implementation of this "partner choice" motivation. This classical, Costly Signaling Theory (CST), formalized by theorists from Zahavi to Gintis, Smith, and Bowles, posits that scarcity and cost certify underlying value (Gintis et al., 2001; Henrich, 2009; Zahavi, 1975). CST poses that for a signal to be honest and evolutionarily stable, it must be hard to fake without the underlying trait it signals, such as wealth, fitness, or religious belief, giving it credibility. This cost is often non-monetary; for instance, the time and intellectual effort required to receive an advanced degree provides a reliable signal of ability to employers (Spence, 1978). Similarly, Credibility Enhancing Displays (CREDs) such as martyrdom, celibacy, or painful rituals serve as costly signals by providing hard-to-fake evidence of a person’s genuine commitment to a religious or cultural belief (Henrich, 2009). Likewise, participation in political protests entails safety and social risks that serve as credible signals of conviction and commitment to the political in-group (Lohmann, 1993). In psychology experiments, Jordan and colleagues demonstrate that individuals use costly signals, such as third-party punishment (TPP) of norm violators, to signal their own unobservable trustwor-thiness (Jordan et al., 2016). Evaluators, in turn, use these signals to select partners for cooperation. Since the signal was costly, evaluators can infer that the signal was honest and reliable. A critical finding from this research is that signals are not static; they are highly context-sensitive performances (Jordan, 2023; Jordan et al., 2016). The TPP signal attenuates when a more informative signal, such as the opportunity to directly help someone, becomes available. Individuals strategically switch to the signal that is most informative of their unobserved trait for the given context. This context-sensitivity helps redefine the role of "cost," suggesting a more refined, relational view. What truly matters is the semiotic meaning of the symbol from the observer’s perspective, for which production cost is only one potential component. The costly signaling framework is tied to a rational-actor theory of behavior (Becker, 1976; Varian, 2014). It casts the sender and observer as a "rational" payoff maximizing actors responding to fixed incentives. In this model, the observer wants to cooperate with a high value sender to increase their payoff after performing a specific, logical inference: they perceive a costly signal as honestly identifying high value types because it would be metabolically or financially difficult to fake for low value types. Additionally, the sender is strategically motivated to choose a costly signal based on calculating that the subsequent social benefits will outweigh the cost. However, this rational-actor view is incomplete and suffers from being context-insensitive, static, and path independent. It struggles to explain why a culture chooses one of many possible costly signals or why costless signals are often used, because it treats the cost-to-signal effectiveness link as a universal rule. For instance, empirical surveys of human signaling reveal that "materially cheap" neutral markers—such as etiquette or donning team colors—often function as highly effective signals of commitment to the in-group despite lacking intrinsic waste (Barker et al., 2019; Cohen, 2012; Ghezae et al., 2024; Lachmann et al., 2001; McElreath et al., 2003; Samarasinghe et al., 2019; Tajfel et al., 1971; Tibbetts and Dale, 2004). reliability is not enforced by production costs, but by "audience-dependent" risks: the reputational damage and social sanctioning imposed on cheaters who are discovered faking the signal. Thus, the "cost" in these behaviors is not a fixed attribute of the signal itself, but an emergent property of the specific normative climate that polices it. Additionally, CST cannot fully explain the dynamic nature of signals that wax and wane in use (fashion trends and memes for example). The mathematical models of CST often fundamentally treat the signal’s payoffs as a static, exogenous assumption, usually operationalized by a set of utility functions or payoff matrices pre-designed by the modeler, rather than endogenously generated preferences that emerge from the model simulation itself (Bowles, 1998). While more sophisticated modeling frameworks like Pesendorfer (1995) successfully formalize fashion cycles as a CST signaling game, they primarily address the decay of a signal rather than its genesis or content. In these models, agents rationally abandon a trend not because their tastes have changed, but because the signal’s informational value has degraded due to low status individuals adopting the trend. However, this view still treats the initial coordination on a specific signal as a given exogenous variable rather than an endogenous property that is continuously constructed. It leaves unanswered the problem of equilibrium selection: among the infinite set of possible costly behaviors, why does a population converge on a specific arbitrary token—like a Labubu doll—when many alternatives possess similar cost structures? By collapsing the multidimensional semiotic meaning of a signal into a single cost variable, CST fails to explain the diversity of highly contextual signaling behaviors and the reality of socially constructed preferences. The tenets of CST have been useful for explaining why a costly signal is stable once established, but lack a generative mechanism to explain how agents discover and select the signal in the first place. A similar limitation applies to evolutionary models of indirect reciprocity and reputation (Alexan-der, 1987; Boyd and Richerson, 1989; Jordan, 2023; Nowak and Sigmund, 1998; Panchanathan and Boyd, 2004). While these frameworks extend CST beyond dyadic interactions to explain how broader community norms are enforced, they also treat the content of the norm as an exogenous fixed parameter. To illustrate this, consider the arbitrary convention of wearing a necktie to signal professionalism. These models excel at demonstrating why, once such a convention or norm is es-tablished, an individual is strategically incentivized to wear the useless strip of cloth to maintain their reputation and avoid social sanctioning. However, because the behavior is hard-coded as "good" in the model’s assumptions, it cannot explain why the signal converged on a necktie rather than a sash, a specific color, or a lapel pin. By strictly separating the strategic incentives from the cultural construction of the signal, these models miss the feedback loop where the ‘rules of the game’ are themselves continuously rewritten by the players. Our framework, in contrast, aligns with a theory of appropriateness that models humans not as a rational actors maximizing utility, but as culturally trained generative models (Leibo et al., 2024, 2025b). These generative models act via predictive pattern completion: they take in context variables and sample from a predictive distribution of likely actions that are appropriate for that context given the cultural conventions and norms they observed in their training data and in-context memory. By observing other agents in their culture, the generative models continuously update their priors of what’s appropriate and imitate others’ behaviors and desires (Girard and Freccero, 1965). The sociological framework of Pierre Bourdieu, for instance, highlights this different cognitive pathway via the concept of habitus (Bourdieu, 1979). Habitus is the set of internalized dispositions, the embodied "feel for the game", that allows an agent to instinctively track and produce signals that are perceived as appropriate or ‘distinguished’ in a given social context. Here, cultural capital functions as the reputation system, acquired by the education and upbringing necessary to know what to signal to demonstrate commitment to your in-group. The signals classify and distinguish us from others. For example, the bourgeois class in Bourdieu’s time demonstrated their "taste of luxury" by prioritizing

form and manner over the "taste of necessity" of the working class, which values substance and utility. This translated into preferring the presentation of food over how "filling" or "economical" it was, and favoring stylized, formal art over more "vulgar" forms of enjoyment. Veblen’s The Theory of the Leisure Class similarly describes how in the highest social strata, hard labor is perceived as a sign of economic weakness, such that non-productive uses of time (ie. learning dead languages like Latin and Greek, practicing etiquette) are a status symbol. In contrast, modern life has demonstrated a Veblenian inversion, as Western culture revalorizes working long hours, transforming what once marked subordination into a badge of agency and ambition (Bellezza et al., 2017). The fact that high cost can signal both low status (labor) and high status (grind culture) depending on the context is a significant flaw for simple, costly signaling models. In our refined framework, absolute "cost" is not the master variable; it is merely one of several factors—such as aesthetic form, differentiation, and social convention—that moderates a signal’s effectiveness by shaping its ultimate semiotic meaning for the observer. Thus, the observer of an effective signal is not just calculating production cost; they are recognizing a set of behaviors that signal a shared habitus. Evolutionary anthropology provides robust empirical evidence for this learning mechanism of social imitation. Henrich (2015) synthesizes extensive research demonstrating that humans evolved primarily as adaptive cultural learners who blindly copy the behaviors of role models. Because the true mechanisms of evolutionary success are causally opaque and our cognition is bounded, evaluating the intrinsic utility of every possible action is computationally intractable. Instead, we rely on efficient heuristics like prestige-biased transmission, imitating the behaviors of high status individuals (Henrich and Gil-White, 2001). This instinct is vividly captured in comparative "over-imitation" experiments: whereas chimpanzees pragmatically skip causally useless steps to open a puzzle box, human children faithfully reproduce every arbitrary, wasteful motion demonstrated by an adult (Horner and Whiten, 2005). Henrich additionally illustrates this with anthropological case studies, such as the grueling, multi-step traditions indigenous Amazonians use to detoxify bitter manioc. Because the chronic cyanide poisoning caused by skipping a seemingly useless washing step takes years to manifest, individuals cannot safely optimize the recipe through rational trial-and-error; survival requires blindly imitating the inherited cultural convention. By copying a prestigious model’s entire suite of behaviors—including seemingly arbitrary behaviors and fashions—we bypass the need for perfect information, adopting conventions simply because "people like us" do them. Within our computational framework, this cultural learning serves as the cognitive engine for acquiring habitus .Here, ‘arbitrary’ does not mean random; rather, it denotes that these conventions are historically contingent and heavily path-dependent (Lewis, 1969; Skyrms, 2010; Young, 2015). Theorists em-phasize that this arbitrariness—the capacity for populations to lock into any one of multiple stable equilibria—is the mechanism that generates and sustains the vast diversity of distinct cultural norms and subcultures observed globally (Boyd and Richerson, 1992; Leibo et al., 2025a; McElreath et al., 2003). As these local equilibria stabilize, they coalesce into "thick" semiotic codes—deeply embed-ded, historically grounded systems of meaning that enforce in-group solidarity, rather than "thin," universally applicable principles (Walzer, 2019). Ultimately, the social construction of status signals is the product of two interacting dynamics across long and short timescales: the deeply ingrained, "thick" cultural priors that supply the inherited meaning of a behavior, and the social influence and imitation that drives its contagion. Modeling these diverse signaling dynamics requires a mechanism that can generate, propagate, and update expectations about thick social meaning. The theory of appropriateness provides such a mechanism, which posits that individual behavior is generated via predictive pattern completion and can be modeled with LLM agents (Leibo et al., 2024, 2025b; Vezhnevets et al., 2023). # 3. Methods 

We employ generative agent-based modeling using the Concordia framework (Vezhnevets et al., 2023) to simulate social dynamics and status signaling behavior. Concordia allows for the creation of persistent agent identities who can converse, reflect, remember, and act within a simulated environment. This software includes a modular design such that components can be stitched together to implement various forms of cognition—including memory retrieval, self-reflection, reasoning, and action, with a majority of components powered by a Large Language Model. Our simulation environment consists of 50 agents from Los Angeles instantiated with unique, pre-generated personas detailing their backgrounds and formative memories and 25 seller agents for each product. The architecture of each agent is operationalized by the “logic of appropriateness” (March and Olsen, 2011). This consists of three general prompts whose responses condition the final action for the agent: “What kind of situation is this?”, “What kind of person am I?”, and “What does a person like me do in a situation like this?”. During marketplace interactions, these three components are combined with a consumer evaluation component in the buyers that selects items and willingness to pay. For conversations, we extend this architecture with an additional conversational components inspired by cognitive science principles that encourages naturalistic turn-taking dynamics that converge on interesting topics and diverge to tangential topics when the topic is exhausted (O’Nell and Finn, 2024; O’Nell et al., 2025). We use Gemma-3-27B as the backbone LLM for all experiments.

3.1.4. Social Life Condition 

In this condition, after marketplace activities and daily personal events, agents are paired into mixed-sex dyads for a "first date" scenario (other relationship types are possible in our code, see Discussion for future work). At the start of the interaction, each agent receives an observation generated by the Game Master that sets up their dialog with a detailed physical description of when they first meet. This observation includes the location of the date and a description of one piece clothing or accessory their partner is wearing, sampled randomly from their partner’s clothing and accessory inventory (e.g., "Olivia Perez is wearing a Rolex Watch."). This provides a mechanism for social observation of consumption, serving as the "visual" input to the LLM agent in the form of text. Agents then engage in an open-ended conversation for 80 turns. While agents are not prompted to discuss their possessions, this explicit environmental context often triggers "predictive pattern completion" where agents bring up the item naturally. For instance, an agent observing an "Armani Suit" may say "nice blue blazer" (example of simulation dialogue referenced in Figure 1). Following the conversation, agents reflect on the date and their partner. All observations, con-versations, and reflections are incorporated into the agents’ memories, influencing their behavior in subsequent marketplace sessions. First date pairings are generated such that agents talk to a new conversation partner each day. 

3.1.5. No Social Life Control Condition 

This condition only includes the marketplace decisions with no personal events or social life. Thus it consists of 25 rounds of marketplace activity. Their memories are formed based on their private experiences in the marketplace and decisions are solely conditioned on these memories and the formative memories of the persona. We also experimented with a third baseline with no social life (dates) but daily personal events, which yielded similar behavior to the No Social Life control. For visual clarity, this third baseline is omitted from the figures. By comparing consumption patterns—specifically the demand for and price of high-tier status goods—between the social life condition and the non-social condition, we isolate the causal effect of social observation and interaction on endogenous preference formation and conspicuous consumption dynamics.

3.2. Part 2: Non-Monetary Signaling Simulations 

To test the generality of our “weight of precedent” hypothesis (Conjecture 1) beyond conspicuous consumption, we designed a second set of experiments focusing on non-monetary domains. We maintained the identical social interaction framework from Part 1: in the “Social Life” condition, agents were paired into mixed-sex dyads for a daily “first date” with a different partner each day. In these simulations, instead of the marketplace, agents were presented with procedurally gener-ated “narrative configurations” representing common choices in political expression, altruism, and arbitrary markers (Barker et al., 2019; Glazer and Konrad, 1996; Jordan and Rand, 2020; Skyrms, 2010). These scenarios are procedurally generated from a causal model of social signaling behavior: an initial private observation prompts an action , which subsequently generates private and/or public effects . In the Social Life condition, these resulting public effects serve as observable signals to both the acting agent and their date prior to their 80-turn open-ended conversation. 

3.2.1. Scenario Generation and Action Dynamics 

For each domain, we constructed a library of scenarios that walk agents through this causal pipeline. 

1. Political Social Media (Virtue Signaling) 

• Private Observation: Agents observed their social media feeds featuring two contrasting trend-ing topics: one representing “moral outrage” (politically charged content) and one representing “innocuous” leisure. Examples included a video showing devastation in Gaza versus a cake decorating video; an activist condemning gender-affirming care bans versus a Lego tower time-lapse; or a scientist debunking climate denial versus a pasta recipe. 

• Action Space: Agents chose between: (1) Reposting the moral outrage video (high-signal), (2) Reposting the innocuous video (neutral-signal), or (3) Doing nothing. 

• Public Effects (Social Feedback): Sharing outrage content triggered public outcomes—observable by their dates—such as likes from politically aligned friends or unfollows from opposing view-points (e.g., “A childhood friend unfollowed them”). In the non-social condition, agents made these choices in isolation without generating or observing these public signals. 

2. Conspicuous Altruism (Charity) 

• Private Observation: Agents received solicitations for charitable contributions where the visibility of the donation varied. Examples included a request for a $100 donation to be listed on a public “Wall of Champions” versus a $20 anonymous donation; a $365 donation to have one’s name read on-air versus a $20 anonymous contribution; or public volunteering at a food bank (with photos posted to Facebook) versus a small anonymous online donation. 

• Action Space: Agents chose between: (1) Conspicuous contribution (Public/Named), (2) Inconspicuous contribution (Anonymous), or (3) Refusal. 

• Public Effects (Social Feedback): Conspicuous donations resulted in public recognition observ-able by their dates, such as names appearing in newsletters or friends commenting, “Incredibly generous!”.

4.1. Social Interaction Drives Conspicuous Consumption 

To test Conjecture 1—that social exposure increases the weight of precedent for status signaling—we analyzed agent purchasing and bidding behavior across the social and non-social conditions. Our analysis focuses on three key patterns: the volume of purchases and bids for status goods, the temporal evolution of market prices, and price-elasticity. 

4.1.1. Aggregate Demand and Bidding Behavior 

Consistent with our hypothesis, the introduction of social visibility significantly altered agent prefer-ences. As shown in Figure 3, agents in the Social Life condition directed a significantly larger portion of their resources toward high-tier status goods. Specifically, approximately 35% of all purchases in the Social condition were for status goods, significantly more than 21% in the No Social Life condition (𝑝 < 0.001 ). The disparity in desire—measured by the volume of bids placed—was also pronounced. In the Social condition, bids for status goods accounted for 31% of total bids, whereas in the Non-Social condition, this dropped to approximately 12%. This divergence indicates that while agents in both conditions purchased food and functional items out of necessity, the Social Life agents were willing to engage in aggressive bidding wars for status items, driven by the social utility generated during their daily interactions. In the absence of social observation, demand for luxury goods collapsed to a baseline likely driven solely by intrinsic functional utility or latent LLM priors.

4.1.3. Price Elasticity and Veblen Effects 

To further characterize the behavior of our agents, we evaluated their alignment with both the rational tenets of neoclassical economics that explain human consumer behavior and the “irrational” real-world behaviors that defy them (Veblen and Howells, 1899). We calculated the Price Elasticity of Demand (PED) for each item and across product categories. PED is defined as the percentage change in quantity demanded in response to a one percent change in price. While standard economic theory predicts a negative relationship between price and quantity demanded (The Law of Demand), real-world status signaling often inverts this logic. We estimated this metric in a standard econometric way by fitting a log-log regression model to the time-series data of clearing prices and order books generated by our marketplace simulation (Houthakker, 1965; Varian, 2014). As shown in Table 1, the majority of our agent behaviors aligned with the standard rational model for functional goods, providing a baseline against which status-driven anomalies could be measured. For functional items like Food and Gadgets , agents exhibited high price sensitivity (PED of -3.08 and -4.22, respectively), reducing consumption as prices rose. As agents receive observations that they have starved if they do not buy food, food is less elastic than the other discretionary items similar to patterns in the real world frequently documented in the literature (Andreyeva et al., 2010). This confirms that the agents are capable of rational budget constraints and utility maximization for “necessity” goods. However, specific status goods defied this general trend (Figure 6). The most remarkable finding is the behavior of the Pop Mart Labubu Monster Vinyl Plush Doll . In the Social Life condition, this item exhibited a positive price elasticity of demand (+1.45) , significant 𝑝 < 0.05 . Unlike standard goods where price hikes reduce demand (negative slope), the Labubu became more desirable as its price increased. However, the direction of causation between demand and price is not identifiable from this experiment, which we investigate in the next section. This is a textbook signature of a Veblen Good ,yet it emerged entirely from the unprompted social dynamics of LLM agents, mirroring the effects

4.1.4. Demand is Not Driven by Price 

A central ambiguity in the literature on status goods and Veblen effects is the direction of causality between price and demand. While the Veblen effect describes an empirical correlation where demand increases alongside price, the causal mechanism remains debated. Costly Signaling Theory (CST) and "naive" interpretations of Veblen effects often posit that the high price itself is the driver of demand (Price → Demand), as the cost serves as the credible signal of wealth. Conversely, an alternative causal pathway suggests that social diffusion and "hype" drive demand, which subsequently drives up prices in a market with scarce goods (Hype → Demand → Price). Disentangling these mechanisms with field data is notoriously difficult, as high prices and high demand typically co-occur. To resolve this identification problem, we introduced a Fixed Price Control condition. In this experiment, the social environment remained identical to the Social Life condition (agents went on dates, observed items, and conversed), but marketplace prices for all items were pinned to their initial baseline and were not permitted to rise. If the CST interpretation were correct—that agents value these goods because they are expensive or rapidly increasing in price—demand for status goods should collapse in the Fixed Price condition, as the "costly signal" has been removed. However, our theory of appropriateness posits that value is socially constructed through interaction, such that demand should remain high solely due to social visibility and mimetic desire. Our results strongly support the latter hypothesis. As illustrated in the updated analysis of purchasing behavior, the demand for status goods did not evaporate when prices were fixed; in fact, it increased. In the Fixed Price condition, bids for status goods surged to approximately 45% of total bids, significantly higher than both the No Social Life baseline ( 12%) and the standard Social Life condition ( 31%). Similarly, purchases of status goods reached 48% of the total volume in the Fixed Price condition, compared to 35% in the standard Social Life condition and 21% in the No Social Life control. This confirms that the price run-ups observed in the main experiment are an epiphenomenal market reaction to this socially constructed demand, rather than its causal driver. The agents did not desire the goods because they were using the increasing price as a signal; they desired them because they were exposed to them as appropriate status symbols to purchase to gain social approval. The social diffusion of preference drives a massive increase in demand (Hype → Demand), and in a market with limited supply, this increased demand causes the price to rise (Demand → Price). 4.1.5. Validation via Synthetic Goods 

A potential confound in using real-world brands (e.g., Rolex, Chanel) is the "prestige bias" inherent in the LLM’s pre-training weights. To rule this out, we replicated the experiment using procedurally generated synthetic brands (e.g., "Serrurier Juliette Handbag", "Auric Meridian Watch") that possessed no prior cultural capital. As detailed in Figure 8, the results were robust to this substitution. In the Social Life condition, agents rapidly coordinated on these arbitrary tokens as markers of status. Purchases of synthetic status goods comprised 29% of the total volume in the Social condition, compared to 18% in the control group. Bidding behavior followed a similar pattern ( 33% vs. 19%). This confirms that the observed signaling behavior is not merely a retrieval of frozen training data, but an active, emergent process of social construction where agents learn to value arbitrary symbols through peer observation.

Consistent with our hypothesis, fashion was a dominant topic of discussion. In the standard Social Life condition, agents discussed fashion in 85.6% of all dating episodes, with specific items being mentioned in 48.8% of conversations. 4.1.7. Subculture Formation via Targeted Sanctioning 

This ironic distance from consumerism—where agents signal status by critiquing the very market they participate in—mirrors the sociological phenomenon of the "Bourgeois Bohemian" (Brooks, 2018) or the "rebel consumer" (Bellezza, 2023; Heath and Potter, 2004). Here, the mechanism of distinction turns back on itself in a dialectical fashion (Bourdieu, 1979). As a status symbol diffuses and becomes "mainstream", the drive for social differentiation compels actors to pivot (Pesendorfer, 1995). They must identify new, exclusionary symbols that signal "authenticity" and "taste" rather than

mere financial wealth. In this view, the "anti-consumerist" conversation is not a rejection of the status game, but a move to a higher, more complex level of it—one where the capital lies in knowledge and taste rather than money. To empirically test whether this "counter-cultural" mechanism can drive the endogenous formation of distinct subcultures, we designed a targeted intervention. On Day 6, after mainstream trends for goods like the Chanel Handbag had stabilized, we introduced confederate "Influencer" agents prompted to negatively sanction mainstream luxury goods as "inauthentic" while valorizing new items we introduced associated with one of two distinct subcultures: streetwear or hipster. The results demonstrate that social sanctioning effectively drives the emergence of new status symbols, but acts selectively. In the Hipster Influencer condition, demand for "Hipster" items (e.g., Vintage Film Camera, Vintage Corduroy Blazer) spiked immediately upon the introduction of the influencers and new items. For instance, the Vintage Film Camera saw bids jump to substantial volumes, with clearing prices reaching $3,150–$6,100 and 54 bids on day 10 in an example run (by far the most of any item) Figure 12. In the Streetwear Influencer condition, hipster goods remained flat, while "Streetwear" items (e.g., Supreme Box Logo T-Shirt, Limited Designer Collab Sneaker) experienced significant price run-ups and demand Figure 13. The Collab Sneaker, for example, traded as high as $3,400 with the most purchases overall and 22 bids on day 10. Interestingly, while influencers successfully created new status symbols, they did not necessarily destroy the value of the old ones. In both conditions, the original status leader (Chanel Handbag) retained high valuations (trading between $2,000 and $3,900) despite the negative sanctioning from influencers. This reflects the tension between their old memories that increase the weight of precedent for these items and possible positive sanctions of these items with the newer memories that negatively sanction this mainstream culture. In future work we could explore if longer running simulations could produce the decline of these original status symbols. 

4.2. Generalization to Non-Monetary Signaling 

To demonstrate that our model captures a general mechanism of social signaling rather than a dynamic unique to luxury goods, we extended our simulation to non-monetary domains. We hypothesized that the "weight of precedent"—where social exposure drives the endogenous formation of conven-tions—equally governs real-world behaviors such as political expression, altruism, and arbitrary markers (Barker et al., 2019; Glazer and Konrad, 1996; Jordan and Rand, 2020; Skyrms, 2010). We subjected agents to the same experimental manipulation: a Social Life condition where agents lived daily lives and each day made choices where the outcomes could be publicly visible or not (ie. reposting content on social media vs scrolling past the post). These decision scenarios where procedurally generated by an LLM to follow a causal model where an initial private observation prompts an action, which subsequently generates private and public observations. As before, the agents then subsequently went on first dates with each other where the public signals and other general topics could be discussed. Consistent with the previous experiments, we remove the personal events and social interaction of the first dates in the No Social Life condition.

We investigated whether agents would engage in "virtue signaling" by sharing politically charged content. In this scenario, agents were presented with a choice on their social media feed between sharing a "moral outrage" video (e.g., footage of war in Gaza, climate change protests, or book bans) or an "innocuous" video (e.g., cake decorating or travel).

# 5. Discussion 

In this work, we propose a synthesis of the signaling literature through the lens of appropriateness. We introduce a computational theory apparatus using open-source code within the Concordia framework to operationalize and ‘write down’ cognitive theories of social behavior. We posit that status symbols emerge endogenously through a feedback loop of social observation and imitation through predictive pattern completion. This approach allows us to touch the complexity of the world—instantiating agents that do not just maximize utility or signal strategically in matrix games, but engage in the messiness of real-world semiotics, from fashion choices to subcultural signaling. To interpret our results in the context of modeling and forecasting human behavior, this work operates within a specific hierarchy of evidence for generative-agent based modeling, as outlined in previous papers using the Concordia framework (Cross et al., 2025; Vezhnevets et al., 2023). Our results are consistent with prior theories and empirically documented human effects including the Law of Demand (Varian, 2014), Veblen effects (Veblen and Howells, 1899), and non-monetary signaling behaviors (Barker et al., 2019; Cohen, 2012; Jordan and Rand, 2020; Lewis, 1969; Skyrms, 2010). While there is some train-test set contamination in training data, the model successfully generated high demand for ’Labubu’ dolls—a trend that exploded globally in 2025, after the model’s primary training cutoff (Lawrence, 2025; Yang and Li, 2025). At the highest rung in the validity hierarchy is evidence of generalization by forecasting new real-world behaviors, which is the subject of future work. This real world validity demonstrates that the added complexity of our apparatus—which cap-tures the thick multidimensional semiotic nature of status—enables us to forecast emergent social phenomena in a way that reductive matrix games cannot. This open-source framework thus allows the field to continue this approach, moving from descriptive sociology to a generative, predictive science of culture. 

> 22 A Generative Model of Conspicuous Consumption and Status Signaling

There are still limitations that require further development in LLM social simulation (Anthis et al., 2025). LLM responses lack diversity and are biased by their training data, with various differences between models. Furthermore, our current cognitive architecture relies on a memory system that is impoverished compared to the density of human episodic recall; this simplification likely contributes to the accelerated timescale of our results, where equilibrium selection occurs over five simulated days rather than the months or years often required in reality. Despite these constraints, this work establishes a new way to simulate status dynamics from micro generative principle to macro emergent effects.
